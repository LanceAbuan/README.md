After 3 years as a Software Developer IV at Paycom, I've learned a lot about building software at scale. But it's the side projects — the chess engines, the AI experiments — that keep me genuinely excited about code.

## What Enterprise Taught Me

At Paycom, I worked on some seriously impactful projects:

- **Custom Fields Engine** — Designed and launched a scalable custom fields system for an enterprise HR platform, improving flexibility and historical data tracking
- **Frontend Migration** — Led a large-scale migration across 20+ teams and 200+ stakeholders, delivering on schedule
- **Data Migration** — Engineered a solution enabling audit-compliant onboarding of enterprise clients generating $1M+ in annual recurring revenue

These projects taught me that great software isn't just about clever algorithms — it's about reliability, maintainability, and working with people.

## The Tech Stack

My daily tools include PHP, TypeScript, React, Python, Java, and C#. On the infrastructure side: Docker, Kubernetes, MySQL, Git, Jira, and Splunk. I've become comfortable picking up whatever the problem requires.

## Why I Still Build Chess Engines

Enterprise work is fulfilling, but there's something magical about building a system that teaches itself. When I started Fool's Gambit, I wanted to understand AlphaZero from the inside out. Every component — the MCTS, the neural network, the training loop — I built from scratch.

The difference between enterprise and passion projects isn't the difficulty. Both are hard. It's that with passion projects, I get to choose the problems I'm obsessed with.

## What's Next

I'm interested in the intersection of AI and practical software development. How can language models, reinforcement learning, and intelligent systems make the tools we build every day fundamentally better? That's where I'm heading.
